## Evaluation

### A. Match with CV

**Fit: Moderate** (3.5/5)

Your full-stack experience technically qualifies you, but Parsons' work is fundamentally different from your background.

**Where it aligns:**
- You have production software shipping experience (PatentIQ, Groundwork, Simplerfy)
- Full-stack architecture knowledge
- Remote-friendly background (worked remote at PersonifyApp)

**Where it diverges:**
- Parsons is defense/government contracting — classified projects, export controls, legacy systems
- Your experience is consumer SaaS and startups
- Their tech stack is often older (Java, C++, enterprise frameworks, not Next.js/FastAPI)
- Government work prioritizes compliance, not iteration speed

**Real talk:** Parsons might say "this resume is all venture tech" and worry about cultural fit or if you'll handle slower processes.

### B. North Star Alignment

**Fit: Weak** (2.8/5)

**Mismatch:**
- Parsons is enterprise + government contracts
- Your stated interests: "fast iteration, learning culture, modern tech stack"
- Government work is the opposite: slow cycles, process-heavy, strict compliance

Your archetypes (Full-Stack Software Engineer, AI/ML Engineer) don't align with defense contracting. You could work there, but it's not where you'll be excited.

**Why you might consider it:**
- Stability (defense budgets are guaranteed)
- Security clearance (valuable, opens future opportunities)
- Remote-friendly

**Why you probably shouldn't:**
- You want to learn new tech. Parsons maintains legacy systems.
- You like shipping fast. Government has slow approval processes.
- Your superpower is "abstraction mastery" across modern stacks. Parsons values different skills.

### C. Compensation

**Fit: Poor** (2.0/5)

The salary range on the posting ($59K-$176K) is misleading. For entry-level, Glassdoor shows:
- Intern salary: ~$59K
- Entry-level full-time: ~$75-95K

**Your target:** $80K minimum. This is below-market entry-level, and you have better options.

Parsed: Parsons pays 30-40% less than Twitch or NVIDIA. Even accounting for government stability, you're taking a pay cut for work that doesn't excite you.

### D. Cultural Signals

**Fit: Weak** (3.0/5)

**Concerns:**
- Defense contractor culture is risk-averse (opposite of startup scrappiness)
- Export controls on technology (you can't share work publicly, bad for portfolio)
- Legacy codebase maintenance (not "building products," more "keeping systems running")
- Bureaucratic approval processes (slow iteration)

**Positives:**
- Job security
- Benefits are solid
- Clearance opportunity
- Remote-first

### E. Red Flags

**Fit: Moderate** (3.0/5)

1. **Tech stack uncertainty.** You can't see their actual stack from the posting. Defense contractors often use older tech (Java Spring, C++ on Windows, databases you've never touched). Risk of skills atrophy.

2. **Portfolio impact.** Government contracts are classified — you can't show your work to future employers. Bad for career trajectory (interviewers will ask "tell us about a system you built" and you can't talk about it).

3. **Organizational inertia.** Parsons has 17,000+ employees. Decision-making is slow. Contrast with PatentIQ (14-person team, week-to-week shipping). This will feel like working in sand.

4. **US Citizenship requirement (likely).** You're a US Citizen, so this is not an issue for you, but it suggests security clearance work (classified).

5. **Vague posting.** The job description is generic and doesn't specify what you'd actually work on. This is common for defense work (can't disclose projects) but makes evaluation hard.
