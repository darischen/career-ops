## E. Red Flags & Wins

**Red Flags:**
1. **Career specialization.** Hardware engineering is deep and narrow. If Daris later wants to pivot to startups, software-heavy companies, or non-hardware roles, this experience is less portable.
2. **Longer ramp-up.** Expect 6-12 months to be productive on IBM's specific systems (zSystems ISA, Power architecture, quantum control systems).
3. **Slower iteration.** Hardware development cycles are longer than software. You might not ship features as quickly as in PatentIQ-style product teams.
4. **Multiple location options but limited flexibility.** The locations are spread across the US (Poughkeepsie, Rochester, Dallas, Austin, Minnesota, NC) — more choice than some roles, but no San Jose / Bay Area option.
5. **Travel needed.** Job says "Some travel may be required based on business demand" — not 100% like consulting, but still a factor.

**Wins:**
1. **Depth opportunity.** Learn CPU/processor/quantum hardware deeply from world-leading team.
2. **Foundational knowledge.** Understanding how hardware works improves all future engineering (performance optimization, memory models, power efficiency).
3. **Prestige.** IBM Systems hardware is legendary. "Worked on zSystems" or "Worked on Power processors" is impressive credential.
4. **Unique differentiation.** Few engineers have deep full-stack (hardware + software + AI) expertise. This makes Daris valuable long-term.
5. **Job security.** Hardware engineers are rarer than software engineers; always in demand.

## F. Interview Prep

**Technical focus:**
1. **HB100 Radar architecture.**
   - Op-amp design choices. Virtual ground biasing rationale.
   - Signal conditioning techniques. Why those filters (Butterworth, Chebyshev)?
   - DSP pipeline (FFT, MUSIC algorithm). Performance metrics.
   - Embedded firmware: real-time constraints, interrupt handling, DMA.

2. **TriCore9 ISA and pipeline.**
   - ISA design choices. Why those instructions?
   - Pipeline stages, hazard detection, forwarding logic.
   - Assembler implementation. How did you validate correctness?
   - Performance optimization. Any critical path analysis?

3. **Digital logic fundamentals.**
   - 16-bit adder: why carry look-ahead? Trade-offs vs. ripple carry?
   - Timing analysis. How did you approach corner-case validation?
   - HDL experience (Verilog). Have you used simulation tools?

4. **Familiarity with hardware targets.**
   - IBM zSystems (mainframe processors). What do you know about their architecture?
   - Power processors. RISC architecture differences from x86.
   - Quantum systems. Basic understanding of quantum gates and control systems.

**Behavioral focus:**
1. **Learning trajectory.** Emphasize that you're interested in hardware depth but understand you'll learn IBM's specific domains on the job.
2. **System thinking.** Discuss how HB100 required end-to-end thinking (analog + digital + firmware + DSP).
3. **Pragmatism.** Discuss debugging decisions (iterative learning, trade-offs).

**Likely questions:**
- Describe your most complex hardware project.
- Walk me through a digital logic design problem.
- How do you approach debugging hardware (limited observability compared to software)?
- Why are you interested in hardware engineering?
- What aspects of processor design interest you most?
- Tell me about a time you optimized hardware performance.
