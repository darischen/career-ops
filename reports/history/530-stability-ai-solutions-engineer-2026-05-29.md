## Red Flags / Blockers

### PRIMARY ISSUE: Role Mismatch

**The Problem:**
Solutions Engineer ≠ Software Engineer. Here's the distinction:

| Aspect | Software Engineer | Solutions Engineer |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Primary goal** | Build products | Sell products |
| **Customer interaction** | Minimal (PMs gather requirements) | High (pre-sales discovery, demos, customization) |
| **Success metric** | Code quality, shipping speed, system design | Deals closed, customer satisfaction, pipeline velocity |
| **Mindset** | "How do I build this optimally?" | "How do I convince this customer to buy?" |
| **Skills valued** | Architecture, coding, problem-solving | Communication, adaptability, sales fundamentals |

**Your Profile:**
- You're a builder (PatentIQ, Groundwork, FlipperZillow)
- You ship products (3+ customer-facing products in 1 year)
- You architect systems (semantic search, caching, API design)
- You do NOT have pre-sales or customer account experience

**Verdict:** This is a career detour, not a progression. You'd be trading engineering for sales.

---

### SECONDARY ISSUE: New Grad Overqualification in Wrong Direction

**The Problem:**
Most new grads won't get Solutions Engineer offers. It typically requires 3-5 years of software engineering experience first. 

If Stability AI is offering this role to new grads (which is rare), they may be:
1. Lower bar for SEs (less senior/prestigious role)
2. Transitioning internal engineers into SE
3. Building a new early-career SE program

**Check during interviews:**
- "Is this entry-level SE role, or mid-level?"
- "What's the typical trajectory—do SEs move back to engineering or stay in sales?"
- "Why are you looking for new grads vs. experienced engineers?"

**But also:** Even if it's entry-level, it's still pre-sales focused. And that doesn't fit you.
